What is a Mole?

A mole is a unit in chemistry used to measure the amount of substance. A mole represents 6.022 × 1023 (atoms, molecules, etc.).

History of the Mole

The groundwork for the concept of the mole was first mentioned by Avogadro. Avogadro proposed that equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of particles. Later on, Josef Loschmidt, an Austrian teacher, estimated the particles in a mole and named it after Avogadro.

Why is it Important?

The mole allows chemists to count particles by weighing them.

Applications

Moles are used in various fields including chemistry, biology, and pharmacology to quantify substances in reactions and processes.

Timeline

Amedeo Avogadro suggested that equal volumes of different gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of particles.
Josef Loschmidt estimates the number of particles in gas.
Jean Baptiste Perrin coins the term "Avogadro's number" and estimates its value based on Brownian motion studies.
Robert Millikan conducts his oil drop experiments to measure the charge on an electron. This led to a more precise calculation of Avogrado's number.
The International Avogadro Project uses xrays and a highly pure silicon sphere to determine Avogadro's number more precisely
The General Conference on Weights and Measures redefines the mole using a fixed value for Avogadro's constant.
The mole number is redefined, setting Avogadro's number at exactly 6.02214076 × 1023 mol−1.
This defined value of Avogadro's constant (6.02214076 × 1023 mol−1) remains the accepted value.

Supplies Used

Coke:

To find how many atoms are in a can of Coke, we know that one can contains around 355 mL of fluid, which is equal to about 355 grams of fluid. Since Coke is 99% water, we will simplify it as H2O. The molar mass of water, which is the mass in grams of one mole of H2O, is approximately 18.015 g/mol. The molar mass can be found by adding the atomic masses of the elements together. Next, we divide the weight of the Coke, which is 355 grams, by the molar mass (18.015 g/mol), giving us approximately 19.7 moles. Then, we multiply 19.7 by Avogadro's number (6.022 × 1023), giving us approximately 1.18 × 1025 molecules in a can of Coke. Finally, to find the total number of atoms, we multiply by 3 (since there are 3 atoms in H2O), resulting in approximately 3.54 × 1025 atoms in a can of Coke.

Water Bottle:

Next, let's find how many atoms are in a bottle of water. A bottle of water is approximately 500 mL, which is about 500 grams. Since it's mostly water, we will use the same approach as before. The molar mass is still approximately 18.015 g/mol. Dividing the weight of the bottle by the molar mass gives us about 27.8 moles. Multiplying by Avogadro's number results in approximately 1.67 × 1025 molecules of water in a bottle. There are about 5.01 × 1025 atoms in a bottle of water.
